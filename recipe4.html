SAMOSA

A samosa is a popular fried or baked pastry with a savory filling, such as spiced potatoes, onions, peas, or meat, originating from the Middle East and staples in Indian cuisine. Known for its crispy, triangular shape, it is typically served hot with chutneys. The dough is made from flour (maida) and deep-fried

Key Aspects of Samosas:

  • Fillings: Common fillings include mashed boiled potatoes, peas, cumin, ginger, and green chilies. Non-vegetarian options include minced meat or fish.
  • Preparation: The dough is made with all-purpose flour, water, and ghee or oil. It is rolled, cut, filled, and sealed into a cone shape.
  • Cooking Method: Typically deep-fried in vegetable oil until golden brown. For a healthier option, they can be air-fried at for 15–20 minutes, brushed with oil, and flipped halfway through.
  • Variations: In East India (Assam, West Bengal, Bihar), a smaller, popular version is known as singara, which often includes diced potatoes, peanuts, and spices. Hyderabad has lukhmi, a smaller, thicker-crust pastry with meat.
  • Serving: Frequently served as a street food snack accompanied by green (mint) chutney or tamarind chutney
